Hi — I'm Dave, and I created EconomicCrimeHub.org entirely as a personal, independent side project.

It began as a straightforward experiment: I wanted to understand what people meant when they talked about AI-assisted coding, so I used it — together with a great deal of my own editing and structuring — to help build the site you see now. What started as a technical curiosity quickly evolved into something more substantial — a free, non-commercial resource intended to help anyone interested in economic crime get a clearer picture of how it actually works and how the world is trying to fight it.

📋
Crime Typologies
Explanations of common economic crime typologies and red-flag patterns so people can recognise what they're seeing.
🕵️
Operations Simulator
A simple operations simulator to walk through realistic detection and response scenarios hands-on.
🔎
Directory
A growing directory of organisations, regulators, industry bodies, and specialist firms active in the economic crime space.
🎬
The Vault
A curated archive of books, documentaries, films, podcasts, reports, and media that have shaped my understanding of financial crime.

This is not meant to be an authoritative textbook, professional training platform, or official reference. It's simply one person's collection of explanations, tools, and resources that I've found useful — shared openly in the hope that students, early-career professionals, journalists, researchers, compliance practitioners, or anyone curious about economic crime might find value here too.

⚡ On Building This

What has surprised and inspired me most is how much I've learned and how energising the whole process has been. "Vibe coding" this site — letting ideas flow freely with AI help while shaping them into something coherent — has unlocked a ton of creativity. I now have a long list of ideas and features I plan to roll out in the coming days and weeks to make the hub even more useful and engaging.

📚 Sources & References

📖 Typologies & Educational Content

The economic crime typologies and explanatory content on this site draw on publicly available materials from authoritative sources including:

  • Financial Action Task Force (FATF) — Recommendations, guidance papers, mutual evaluation reports, and typology studies
  • FinCEN (Financial Crimes Enforcement Network) — Advisories, SAR filing guidance, and AML/CFT regulations
  • Wolfsberg Group — Correspondent banking principles, guidance on PEPs, and AML standards
  • EU Anti-Money Laundering Directives (AMLD4, AMLD5, AMLD6) and the EU's Anti-Money Laundering Authority (AMLA) framework
  • Basel Committee on Banking Supervision — Sound management of risks related to money laundering and financing of terrorism
  • Transparency International — Corruption Perceptions Index and anti-corruption research
  • ACAMS, ICA, RUSI, Chatham House and other professional and research bodies active in financial crime

All descriptions and explanations are original compositions by the creator. No text has been copied from these or any other sources.

🕵️ Operations Simulator

The simulator scenarios are entirely fictional and created for educational purposes. Case structures, red-flag patterns, and investigation workflows are inspired by publicly available typology reports and educational materials from:

  • FATF typology reports and case studies
  • FinCEN advisories and SAR Activity Review publications
  • Wolfsberg Group guidance on correspondent banking, PEPs, and sanctions screening
  • ACAMS study materials and examination preparation resources
  • The Basel AML Index methodology

All names, entities, banks, companies, trusts, and individuals in the simulator are entirely fictional. No real-world case data, confidential information, or proprietary materials from any employer or organisation have been used.
